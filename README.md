# Layer-wise-Decodability-of-Word-Frequency-in-Decoder-Only-Language-Models
Simple-MultiArc LDT Workflow.jpg


We investigate how word frequency is represented across layers in decoder-only language models. Using frozen hidden states, we probe lexical decision, direct frequency classification, and continuous-frequency regression in a controlled evaluation centered on matched 3B models and extended to a nine-model sweep across the Llama, Qwen, and Smol families. Frequency information is strongly decodable across families and scales: binary high-versus-low probes reach high held-out accuracy, three-way probes support finer low/mid/high discrimination, and regression analyses recover a strong graded relation to continuous log frequency. Across the full sweep, one layer-wise pattern is especially consistent: the \emph{relative} advantage of high-frequency over low-frequency words is largest in the earliest layers and decreases with depth, while frequency remains strongly decodable throughout the network. Token-restricted, reduced-confound, pooling, multi-seed, and probe-selectivity analyses support this interpretation, but do not justify a causal claim about how models use frequency during computation. Overall, the results show that decoder-only language models encode a robust and graded representation of word frequency, with the clearest early-layer signature appearing as stronger relative separability for high-frequency words.
